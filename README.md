
## FROM GLOBAL MODELS TO LOCAL ECOLOGICAL INSIGHTS:
DOWNSCALING LAND USE PROJECTIONS FOR CONSERVATION APPLICATION IN SOUTHERN AFRICA

The dynamics of socio-ecological systems in Southern Africa are increasingly impacted by climate and land use changes. 
It is among the regions most susceptible to climate change: Frequent extreme weather events including increased occurrences 
of floods and droughts have been observed over the past two decades, as well as long-term projections of greater rainfall 
variability under global warming scenarios of 1.5°C and 2°C. In addition to climate stressors, anthropogenic activities 
such as extensive agriculture, deforestation, and urban and infrastructure development are contributing to habitat fragmentation, 
biodiversity loss, and desertification. These changes not only exacerbate poverty but also put substantial pressure 
on ecosystem services, vital for both human communities and wildlife. 

As competition for these limited services and land grows, human-wildlife conflicts (HWC) escalates, 
threatening both species conservation and sustainable human livelihoods. A critical component of understanding and mitigating these 
impacts is ensuring that global land use projections are downscaled to resolutions suitable for species-specific habitat modeling. 
However, coarse-resolution data often fail to capture the fine-scale landscape features and recources critical for many species' survival. 
For example, small mammal habitat use like small canivoures in HERPESTIDAE family or small antelops Nesotragus moschatus `home range size 0.03 km^2`, 
which are highly dependent on microhabitats, cannot be accurately modeled at coarse resolutions. 
Similarly, important landscape features such as wetlands and small water bodies, which might span only a few meters to a few hundred meters, 
are often generalised or replaced with other land use classes at lower resolutions, despite their important role in supporting aquatic and semi-aquatic species.

By downscaling land use projections to finer spatial resolutions, we can more accurately reflect the heterogeneous regional landscapes that species rely on. 
This is particularly important for species with specific habitat requirements, such as those dependent on wetlands, riparian zones, or other small-scale ecosystems, 
which contribute disproportionately to biodiversity. Such precision in land use data allows for more reliable predictions for land use impacts on species distribution, 
habitat availability, and overally on biodiversity. Accurate downscaling not only supports better conservation planning but also improves human-wildlife coexistence 
by identifying critical areas for protection.

Downscaling tools offer the ability to capture these fine-scale dynamics, making them indispensable for conservation strategies aimed at mitigating biodiversity loss.
Here, we make use of a newly developed method, LandScaleR (Woodman et al2023) to downscale global projection dataset from PLUMv2 global land use change model. These will be 
matched on MODIS, SENTINEL and ESA data that is available at high resolusion of 500 m, 100m, and 10 m respectively.
